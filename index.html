A Reflection on Gender Equality

2023 • illustration • Clip Studio Paint

This digital poster series explores women’s rights issues across different decades. Using a pop-art comic style, the work creates bold, eye-catching visuals that draw in the viewer. The first poster represents progress made during the 1970s equal rights movement in the United States. The second highlights ongoing discrimination faced by women in the workforce during the 2000s. The third reflects recent events surrounding the removal of abortion rights, emphasizing how society’s treatment of women’s rights has come full circle. Through narrative, framing, editing, and color symbolism, the series communicates a message about equality and the regression of women’s rights over time.

Color Symbolism

Hover over the colors to discover their meaning in the narrative

Mint Green (1970s)

Represents hope and progress during the Equal Rights Movement. The fresh, optimistic tone reflects the era's belief in positive change and forward momentum.

Deep Maroon (2000s)

Symbolizes the stagnation and corporate suppression of women's rights. The muted, serious tone reflects workplace discrimination and ongoing inequality.

Purple/Lavender (2020s)

The color of feminism and women's movements, now tinged with sadness. Represents the emotional weight of rights being taken away and the despair of regression.

Bold Red Typography

"OVERWORKED" and "UNDERPAID" in striking red demands attention and conveys urgency, anger, and the critical nature of ongoing discrimination issues.

Blue Eyes & Accents

The consistent blue eyes across all panels symbolize the unchanging humanity and shared experience of women, despite different decades and struggles.

Black Ink & High Contrast

Bold black linework in pop-art style creates stark contrasts that emphasize the clear divide between progress and regression, right and wrong.

Design Philosophy: The color palette shifts dramatically across three decades—from hopeful mint green in the 1970s, to oppressive maroon in the 2000s, to somber purple in the 2020s. This progression visually represents the "full circle" regression of women's rights, with each era's mood reflected in its dominant color story.
